Philosophy

How I think about the work.

01

Small details are how teams show they care.

A button radius, a tap target, an empty state. These are how you tell a user you see them. Caring about the small stuff is what design culture actually is, and protecting that when there's pressure to ship is a big part of leading a team.

02

Good work comes from energized teams.

Process and frameworks matter, but trust matters more. My job is to keep designers curious, supported, and close enough to the craft that the work feels like something. I've never seen beautiful design come out of a depleted team.

03

A practice grows in the open.

Designers learn fastest from each other. The community, the podcast, the hackathons, the workshops — these aren't side projects, they're the actual practice. The field gets better when we share work before it's finished and say what we actually think out loud.

Walmart · 2019 — 2021

A design system, built with the people using it.

Migrating Walmart's Living Design System to Figma — a shared resource library serving both designers and developers, with UI kits, documentation, and components built and maintained alongside the teams shipping on them. The library was the easy part; adoption is where most systems die. The dashboard built on top drove a +$27M GMV uplift in three months.

What I learned

A design system serves two audiences — designers and the developers who build from it — and it fails when it's handed down to either. The office hours, the docs, the UI kits iterated alongside the teams using them: that's what made adoption stick across both. (Office hours are also a great way to find out what's actually broken.)

My role

Senior UX Lead on the design systems team. I owned the Sketch → Figma migration, kept the shared component library accurate and well-organized, designed the "Duplo" extra-large button now across self-checkout kiosks, automotive, and car wash screens, and led tag styling standards on walmart.com — partnering closely with engineering throughout.

The dashboard that proved it worked

I built the Offer Listing Quality Dashboard from zero — helping Marketplace sellers find and fix incomplete listings, on the system we'd just shipped. A well-tended system makes the next product faster.

Air India · Pilot Sector Report · iOS · iPad · 2023

From 15 minutes on paper to 2:30 on iPad.

A native iPadOS app for Air India pilots to file flight, incident, and sector reports — replacing paper forms that took 10–15 minutes a shift and then sat 3–4 days waiting to be re-keyed by hand.

Plus a 3–4 day data lag, eliminated. Reports hit the database the moment a pilot taps submit.

The problem

Pilots wrote reports on paper, then waited days for someone to key the data in. Tedious, error-prone, and a bottleneck on operational data the airline needed.

My role

Design lead. I interviewed pilots about the forms and the Airbus/Boeing reporting differences, and mapped the database constraints with stakeholders. The unlock: sector reports didn't have to live inside the larger flight report. Treated as their own object, the whole flow got simpler.

Two pilots, two rhythms

Captain Sameer flies Airbus narrowbodies — 3–4 short flights a day, back-to-back reports. Captain Gaurav flies Boeing widebodies — one long-haul with a big crew and scheduled rest. Same app, very different filing patterns.

The approach

  • Field research with both Airbus and Boeing pilots
  • Concept sketches reviewed with pilots before pixel design
  • Wireframes iterated through several rejected directions

What happened

Every pilot tested completed a submission on their own, unaided. One thing surfaced: some text-only links were styled like icons, and pilots kept tapping the visuals. Fixed that in the next round.

Sprint to Win — a racing game anyone could play.

NYC Marathon Expo · 2019

The problem

Marathons are exciting if you're running one — less so from the sidelines. NYRR wanted something for the expo that let spectators participate, and we wanted it to work for everyone, including the wheelchair athletes the marathon is famous for.

My role

Concept and game mechanics lead. I shaped the core interaction, ran the team through prototyping and on-floor testing, and owned the visual system so the game read instantly in a noisy room.

How we built it

DDR dance mats, a wheelchair treadmill, an iPad for picking your avatar, and a smart TV showing the race — plus a lot of calibration. Pads sensed footsteps, the treadmill sensed pushes, both fed the same race engine — so a kid in sneakers could race a Paralympic athlete and it felt fair. Hacked-together hardware, clean idea: one starting line, two ways to run.

Before each race you picked a 3D avatar — by ethnicity, gender, ability, and style. Letting people see someone like themselves in the race mattered as much to us as the mechanics did.
