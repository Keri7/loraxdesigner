Case 01 — Walmart · Senior UX/UI Lead · 2019–2021

A design system built with the people using it.

Walmart's Living Design System needed to move from Sketch to Figma — and actually get adopted by the designers and engineers shipping on it. I owned the migration, kept the shared component library accurate, and built the dashboard that put the system's value in dollar terms.

+$27M
GMV uplift (+48%) in 3 months
5×
Faster turnaround on new feature releases
11+
Platforms unified, accessibility built in
Fig. 01 — Living Design System · button matrix · every state, every platform

The Challenge

A design system serves two audiences — designers and the engineers building from it — and fails when it's handed down to either one. Adoption, not the library itself, was the hard part.

My Role

Senior UX Lead on the design systems team. I owned the Sketch → Figma migration, designed the "Duplo" extra-large self-checkout button now live across kiosks, automotive, and car wash screens, and led tag styling standards on walmart.com.

What I Shipped

The Offer Listing Quality Dashboard, built from zero, helped Marketplace sellers fix incomplete listings on the system we'd just shipped — 297.5K SKUs updated, +700 BPS quality score improvement.

Why It Worked

Office hours and docs that iterated alongside the teams using them — not handed down — is what made adoption stick across both audiences.

Case 02 — Air India · Pilot Sector Report · Design Lead · 2023

From 15 minutes on paper to 2:30 on iPad.

Air India pilots filed flight, incident, and sector reports on paper — 10–15 minutes a shift, then 3–4 days before the data was re-keyed by hand. I designed the native iPadOS app that replaced it.

The Challenge

Tedious, error-prone paper forms, and a multi-day bottleneck on operational data the airline needed in real time.

My Role

Design lead. I interviewed pilots about the forms and the Airbus/Boeing reporting differences, then mapped database constraints with stakeholders. The unlock: sector reports didn't need to live inside the larger flight report.

The Approach

Field research with both Airbus and Boeing pilots, concept sketches reviewed before pixel design, and wireframes iterated through several rejected directions.

What Happened

Every tested pilot completed a submission unaided. One fix from testing: some text-only links read as icons, so pilots kept tapping the wrong thing — corrected in the next round.

Case 03 — NYC Marathon × TCS Interactive · Concept & Game Mechanics Lead · 2019

A racing game built for every body.

A wheelchair-accessible racing game for the NYC Marathon expo, built with New York Road Runners. Tens of thousands played — kids, grandparents, wheelchair athletes — all on the same starting line.

Red Dot
Best of the Best, 2019

Fig. 01 — Two pressure pads, one finish line — designed inclusively from the first sketch.

The Challenge

NYRR wanted something for the expo that let spectators participate — and we wanted it to work for everyone, including the wheelchair athletes the marathon is famous for.

My Role

Concept and game mechanics lead. I shaped the core interaction, ran prototyping and on-floor testing, and owned the visual system so the game read instantly in a noisy room.

How We Built It

DDR dance mats and a wheelchair treadmill fed the same race engine, so a kid in sneakers could race a Paralympic athlete and it felt fair. Before each race, players picked a 3D avatar by ethnicity, gender, ability, and style.

Approach

How I work.

01

Small details, protected.

A button radius, a tap target, an empty state — sweating these is what design culture actually is, especially under pressure to ship.

02

Trust over process.

Frameworks matter, but trusted, energized teams are what actually produce great work. I've never seen good design come out of a depleted team.

03

Complexity is a design problem.

The most valuable thing a design team can do in a technical domain is make the hard stuff feel obvious. That takes deep collaboration with engineers and PMs — and the patience to learn the domain before you try to simplify it.
